Demonetisation and GST: Perils of Hasty Reform
By Manish Sisodia

Live Session by Manish Sisodia on GST

The Indian economy has been celebrated for being remarkably resilient. In the face of the 2008 global financial meltdown, India did not figure in the list of dominoes that fell. The effects of the global recession did not appear to impact India’s economy to the extent it impacted several large economies that are fully integrated with the global market. Ever since the 2008 crisis, the Indian economy more or less recovered the setbacks it suffered at the time. But the Union government’s decision to demonetise high-value currency in November last year has had a crippling effect, which has for the first time in several years led to the GDP growth rate to fall year-on-year so sharply. No matter the stated purpose of the move, in hindsight, a large majority of economists agree that demonetisation was a big blow to the economy.

I fear the Goods and Services Tax (GST) regime could be another such blow to our economy. Before I go into what is fuelling my concern, let me state this clearly that the Aam Aadmi Party has always supported the GST in principle. In fact, Delhi government has made every effort to assist and support Delhi’s traders and businessmen in adapting to this new tax regime.In a Facebook Live session last month on this issue—the first such interaction by any Finance Minister in the country—I took questions from the people of Delhi and addressed their concerns with the implementation of GST in Delhi..

However, GST in its present form suffers from severe flaws. One Nation - One Tax is an excellent idea that has successful models across the world. But what we are bringing to India is One Nation - Multiple Slabs of Taxes. Moreover, the fact that we will be taxing several items, including some commonly used goods, at a steep 28% rate is alarming and defies logic. GST was supposed to mean lower taxes, instead we got irrationally high tax rates.

When an average Indian buyer is shopping for small appliances, and purchases items worth Rs 10,000, they will have to shell out an additional Rs 2,800 as GST. Smaller stores will ask consumers, “Do you want a pucca bill or not?” and when they realise it will be much cheaper to purchase appliances in black, they are likely to choose that option. Citizens want to be honest taxpayers, but the government must also have fair taxation. With a 28% tax rate on some items, evasion will be the most preferred choice. Why can’t there be a flat 12% GST, shared 50-50 between Centre and states? Such a tax rate would give a major boost to the economy. It will encourage consumption but also blunt inflation by slashing tax on many essential items.

AAP believes that an optimal tax rate should create an incentive for compliance. The Delhi government demonstrated it successfully over the last two years that lower the tax, the higher the compliance and overall revenue collection. Over the last three Budgets I have presented as Delhi’s Finance Minister, VAT on several items that are essential for the aam aadmi has been lowered from 12.5% to 5%, while that on some luxury items has been hiked. Despite not hiking VAT on any essential item, Delhi’s VAT collection surged by 10.7% in 2015-16 as compared to a 2% rise the previous year.

The items in the various slabs of GST are also arbitrary in many cases. For instance, it is bizarre that a farmer would pay as much tax on tractor parts that one would pay on a luxury SUV. The GST Council made special efforts to slash GST on agarbattis over the last month, but that on sanitary pads is untouched. I am hopeful that the GST Council will rationalize some of the glaring discrepancies in the slabs and in the months to come people will have a slightly more fair system..

My third and most critical concern over the implementation of GST was fuelled by an interview of Navin Kumar, Chairman of the GST Network, published by a leading financial daily earlier this week. The headline of the interview read, ‘No time for software testing now’. Mr Kumar also confirmed a lot of fears about the preparedness of the IT infrastructure needed for the smooth rollout of GST from 1st July onward. It is not a laughing matter that the Chairman has admitted in all seriousness that the GSTN may not have any time left to even test the backend systems central to GST’s implementation.
